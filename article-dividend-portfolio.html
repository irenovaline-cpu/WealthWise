What Is Dividend Investing?

When you own shares of a company, some companies pay you a portion of their profits on a regular schedule ‚Äî usually quarterly. This payment is called a dividend. Dividend investing means building a portfolio specifically designed to maximize this income stream.

Unlike selling stocks for a profit (which requires market timing), dividends arrive whether the market is up or down. A company like Johnson & Johnson has raised its dividend every single year for over 60 consecutive years ‚Äî through recessions, pandemics, and market crashes.

1

Dividend Yield

The annual dividend payment divided by the share price. A $100 stock paying $4/year has a 4% yield. This is your "interest rate" from owning the stock.

2

Dividend Growth Rate

How much the company increases its dividend each year. A 7% grower doubles its payout every 10 years ‚Äî without you buying more shares.

3

DRIP (Dividend Reinvestment)

Automatically reinvesting your dividends to buy more shares. This is the engine of compounding ‚Äî the single most powerful move a beginner investor can make.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Dividend investing is simple, but not easy. The biggest mistakes beginners make have nothing to do with stock selection ‚Äî they're behavioral. Here's what to watch out for:

Chasing high yields. A 12% dividend yield sounds incredible until you realize the stock has dropped 50% in price and the dividend is about to be cut. Sustainable yields are typically 2%‚Äì6%. Anything higher deserves serious scrutiny.

Selling during a dip. When the market drops, your portfolio value falls but your dividend income often doesn't. A company paying $4/share in dividends will likely keep paying $4/share even if the stock price drops from $100 to $80. Stay the course and keep reinvesting.

Ignoring taxes. Dividends are taxable income. Qualified dividends are taxed at the capital gains rate (0%‚Äì20% depending on your income), which is favorable. Holding dividend stocks inside a Roth IRA eliminates taxes on this income entirely ‚Äî a major advantage to explore.

üí° Pro Tip ‚Äî Use a Roth IRA: If you're eligible, hold your dividend portfolio inside a Roth IRA. Your contributions grow tax-free, and all dividend income and withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. This alone can add hundreds of thousands of dollars to your lifetime results.
