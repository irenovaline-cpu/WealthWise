Index Funds vs ETFs: What's the Difference and Which Should You Buy?

A plain-English breakdown of the two most popular passive investing vehicles for beginners.

‚è± 8 min read ¬∑ By WealthWise Team ¬∑ February 2025 ¬∑ Free
If you've started researching investing, you've seen both terms everywhere. Index funds and ETFs are often used interchangeably ‚Äî but they're not the same thing. Understanding the difference will help you choose the right vehicle for your specific situation.

What Is an Index Fund?

An index fund is a type of mutual fund that tracks a specific market index ‚Äî like the S&P 500, which represents the 500 largest US companies. Instead of a fund manager trying to pick winning stocks, the fund simply buys every stock in the index in proportion to its market size.

The result: you get instant diversification across hundreds of companies with a single purchase, and because there's no active management, the fees (expense ratios) are extremely low ‚Äî often 0.03% to 0.20% per year. Vanguard's VFIAX (S&P 500 index fund) has an expense ratio of just 0.04%.

What Is an ETF?

An ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund) is also a fund that tracks an index ‚Äî but unlike a mutual fund, it trades on a stock exchange like a regular share. You can buy and sell ETFs throughout the trading day at market prices, just like buying Apple or Tesla stock.

Vanguard's VOO is an ETF that tracks the same S&P 500 index as VFIAX. The performance is virtually identical. The difference is in how and when you can buy them. ETFs often have lower minimum investment requirements ‚Äî you can buy one share of VOO for around $400, while some index funds require $1,000‚Äì$3,000 minimums to start.

The Key Differences That Actually Matter

Trading flexibility: ETFs trade in real time during market hours. Index funds price once per day after market close. For long-term investors, this distinction is irrelevant ‚Äî but it matters to traders.

Minimum investment: Most ETFs have no minimum beyond the price of one share. Many index funds require $1,000‚Äì$3,000 to open. This makes ETFs more accessible for beginners.

Automatic investing: Index funds make it easy to set up automatic monthly investments for exact dollar amounts (like $200/month). ETFs require fractional shares support from your broker to do this automatically.

Tax efficiency: ETFs are generally slightly more tax-efficient than index funds due to how they handle redemptions, though the difference is minimal in tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs.

Which Should You Buy?

For most beginners, the answer is ETFs ‚Äî specifically broad market ETFs like VOO (S&P 500), VTI (Total US Market), or VXUS (International). They're accessible with any amount, have ultra-low fees, and are available on every major brokerage platform including M1 Finance, Fidelity, and Schwab.

If you're investing through a 401k or similar employer plan, you'll likely only have access to index funds ‚Äî and that's perfectly fine. Both vehicles achieve the same goal: low-cost, diversified exposure to the market. Don't let the terminology stop you from investing. Pick one and start.
