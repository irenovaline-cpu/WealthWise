Should You Invest Internationally? The Case for Global Diversification

Why putting all your money in US stocks is riskier than most people think and how to fix it.

‚è± 9 min read ¬∑ By WealthWise Team ¬∑ February 2025 ¬∑ Free
The US stock market has been the best performing major market in the world for the past decade. Many investors have concluded this means they should put 100% of their money in US stocks. This is a recency bias mistake ‚Äî and understanding why will make you a more resilient investor.

The US Is Only 60% of the World's Stock Market

The global stock market is roughly $100 trillion. The US represents about $40 trillion ‚Äî 40-45% of the total. Investing only in US stocks means ignoring more than half of all investable companies on earth.

Those non-US companies include some of the world's most valuable businesses: ASML (semiconductor equipment), LVMH (luxury goods), Novo Nordisk (diabetes drugs), Samsung (electronics), Nestl√© (consumer staples), and Toyota (automotive). Many of these companies have better growth profiles, stronger dividends, or cheaper valuations than their US equivalents.

What History Shows About US vs International Performance

The 2010-2020 decade was extraordinary for US stocks. The S&P 500 returned roughly 13% annually. International stocks (MSCI EAFE index) returned about 6% annually. This gap has led many investors to declare US stocks permanently superior.

But look at the 2000-2010 decade: the S&P 500 returned -0.9% annually (the lost decade). International stocks returned +2.6%. Investors who held only US stocks lost money. Those with global diversification stayed positive. Market leadership rotates. Nobody knows when, which is why holding both is logical.

How to Add International Exposure

The simplest approach: add VXUS (Vanguard Total International Stock ETF) to your portfolio. It covers 7,900+ stocks across 46 countries in developed and emerging markets. Expense ratio: 0.07%.

Suggested allocations: Conservative global ‚Äî 70% US (VTI) / 30% International (VXUS). Moderate global ‚Äî 60% US / 40% International. This matches roughly how these markets compare by size.

Alternatively, VT (Vanguard Total World Stock ETF) does it automatically ‚Äî it holds both US and international stocks at their market-weight proportions in a single fund. One ETF, entire world market. Expense ratio: 0.07%.

The Risks of International Investing to Understand

Currency risk: International returns are affected by exchange rates. When the dollar strengthens, international returns shrink for US investors. When the dollar weakens, they're amplified. Over long periods, these effects largely cancel out.

Political and regulatory risk: Emerging markets in particular carry higher political risk than developed markets. Country-specific events can devastate single-country investments. Broad diversification across countries mitigates this significantly.

Higher fees: International ETFs typically cost slightly more than US ETFs ‚Äî 0.07% vs 0.03%. Still extremely cheap. This should not discourage global diversification.
