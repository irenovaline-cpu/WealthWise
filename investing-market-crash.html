What to Do When the Stock Market Crashes

The counterintuitive moves that separate wealthy investors from panicked ones during downturns.

‚è± 9 min read ¬∑ By WealthWise Team ¬∑ February 2025 ¬∑ Free
Every major market crash in history ‚Äî 1929, 1987, 2000, 2008, 2020 ‚Äî was followed by a recovery to new all-time highs. Every single one. The investors who panicked and sold locked in permanent losses. The ones who stayed invested, or better yet bought more, became wealthy. Here's exactly what to do.

First: Do Absolutely Nothing

The most important action during a crash is inaction. Your portfolio dropping 30% is terrifying. Your brain ‚Äî wired for survival ‚Äî interprets financial loss as physical danger and screams at you to run. This instinct is wrong.

Research by Dalbar shows the average investor earns just 3.7% annually over 20 years while the S&P 500 returns 7.2%. The gap is entirely explained by buying high and selling low during panics. The investor who simply held an S&P 500 index fund and did nothing doubled the returns of the average investor who 'actively managed' their portfolio.

Second: Keep Your Automatic Investments Running

If you have automatic monthly investments set up ‚Äî keep them running. This is dollar-cost averaging working exactly as intended. When prices are 30% lower, your same $300 monthly contribution buys 43% more shares than it did at the peak.

Every share you buy at depressed prices becomes a massive winner when the market recovers. The 2020 COVID crash dropped 34% in 33 days. Investors who kept buying in March 2020 saw those purchases more than double by the end of the year. The crash was a gift to anyone who kept investing.

Third: Rebalance Into Stocks

If you have a bond allocation in your portfolio, a crash is the perfect time to rebalance. Bonds hold their value while stocks drop ‚Äî so your allocation naturally shifts toward bonds during a crash. Rebalancing means selling bonds and buying stocks when they're cheap.

This feels deeply counterintuitive. It requires buying the thing everyone is fleeing. But this is systematically buying low and selling high ‚Äî the definition of good investing. Set a rule: if your stock allocation drops more than 10% from your target, rebalance. Remove the emotion by making it mechanical.

What NOT to Do

Do not sell. Ever. Selling converts paper losses into permanent losses and removes you from the recovery. If you sold during the 2008 crash and waited for things to 'feel safe' before reinvesting, you likely missed 2009 and 2010 ‚Äî two of the best recovery years in market history.

Do not check your portfolio daily. Every day of checking during a crash is a day you'll feel the urge to sell. Monthly or quarterly check-ins are sufficient. Do not listen to financial media. Their job is to keep you watching, and fear keeps you watching. Market crash coverage is entertainment, not financial advice.
