Dollar-Cost Averaging: Why Investing the Same Amount Every Month Wins

The strategy that removes emotion from investing and builds wealth automatically through any market.

‚è± 8 min read ¬∑ By WealthWise Team ¬∑ February 2025 ¬∑ Free
Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is the practice of investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals ‚Äî regardless of what the market is doing. $300 on the 1st of every month, rain or shine. It's simple, it's automatic, and research consistently shows it produces better outcomes than trying to time the market.

How Dollar-Cost Averaging Works

Say you invest $300/month into an S&P 500 ETF. In January, the ETF costs $100/share ‚Äî you buy 3 shares. In February, the market drops and the ETF is $75 ‚Äî your $300 buys 4 shares. In March, it recovers to $90 ‚Äî you buy 3.33 shares.

Your average cost per share is $88 ‚Äî lower than the January starting price of $100. You automatically bought more shares when prices were low and fewer when prices were high ‚Äî without making a single active decision. This is DCA working exactly as intended.

DCA vs Lump Sum: Which Wins?

Research from Vanguard found that lump sum investing (putting all your money in at once) outperforms DCA approximately 68% of the time over a 10-year period. The reason: markets go up more than they go down, so being invested sooner is generally better.

However, this assumes you have a lump sum available. For most people building wealth from a paycheck, DCA is the only practical option. And for investors who would otherwise panic and withdraw during crashes, DCA's automatic nature is worth the theoretical underperformance. The best strategy is the one you'll actually stick with.

The Psychological Power of DCA

The greatest benefit of DCA isn't mathematical ‚Äî it's behavioral. By making investing automatic, you remove the biggest threat to your returns: yourself.

When the market drops 30%, DCA investors often feel less panic because they know their next automatic purchase is buying at a discount. The crash feels like an opportunity rather than a catastrophe. This reframe is extremely valuable. Studies show that investors who automate contributions stay invested through downturns at significantly higher rates than those making manual investment decisions.
